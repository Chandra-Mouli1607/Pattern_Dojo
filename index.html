What is CRT?

Every higher-timeframe candle is just a range of price action on a lower timeframe. Candle Range Theory says that range follows a predictable 3-act script — and if you can read it, you can trade the reversal before the crowd realizes the breakout was fake.

The 3-Act Play

  1. 1
    Accumulation: Price chops sideways inside the previous candle's high and low. Orders are being collected quietly.
  2. 2
    Manipulation: Price sweeps the liquidity above or below the range — a false breakout that traps retail traders and triggers their stops.
  3. 3
    Distribution: The real move begins. Price reverses and drives toward the opposite end of the range (or beyond).
CRT 3-Phase Model — Bullish Reversal

The golden rule: when one candle sweeps the previous candle's high or low and price returns inside the range, the real move is toward the opposite extreme.

What is Turtle Body Soup?

TBS is the entry method inside a CRT setup. The name is a play on the original Turtle Traders who traded breakouts — Turtle Soup flips that by fading false breakouts and "making soup out of the turtles."

The "body" part is critical: in TBS, the manipulation candle's body closes beyond the previous high or low. This traps more traders than a wick-only sweep, creating a higher-probability reversal.

TBS (Turtle Body Soup)

Body CLOSES beyond the level. Stronger trap = higher probability. More liquidity grabbed.

TWS (Turtle Wick Soup)

Only the WICK pokes past. Body stays inside. Weaker signal — fewer traders trapped.

How to Find the CRT Candle

You're looking for a 3-candle sequence on your HTF where the middle candle sweeps one side of the range and the third expands to the other.

  1. 1
    Pick your HTF candle — Daily, 4H, or 1H. Mark its high and low — that's your range.
  2. 2
    Watch Candle 2 — Does it break the high or low but close back inside the range? That's your manipulation = CRT trigger.
  3. 3
    Candle 3 = Distribution — Price expands toward the opposite extreme. This is where your trade lives.
CRT 3-Candle Identification — Bullish Setup
Pro Tip

The deeper the sweep wick and the stronger the close back inside the range, the higher probability. A candle that barely tags the level is weak. You want a convincing stab that grabs real liquidity — then a decisive rejection.

How to Find TBS

TBS is your precision entry. The key: the sweep candle's body closes beyond the previous level — not just the wick.

TBS Model #1 — Standard Entry

  2. 2

  3. 3

  4. 4
    Enter at OB/FVG: After MSS, enter on the retest of the OB or FVG created by the reversal.
  5. 5

What is the Silver Bullet?

The Silver Bullet is ICT's time-based sniper strategy. Instead of watching charts all day, you only trade during specific 1-hour windows when institutional algorithms are most active. You wait for a liquidity sweep, spot the FVG left behind, and enter on the retrace. Clean, quick, minimal screen time.

It's designed for scalping — targeting 20-30 pips on major forex pairs, or equivalent moves on indices and crypto. The entire trade from entry to exit often completes within 30-60 minutes.

The 3 Silver Bullet Windows (EST)

3:00–4:00 AM (London) 10:00–11:00 AM (NY AM) 2:00–3:00 PM (NY PM)

The 10:00–11:00 AM NY session is considered the highest probability window. This is when NYSE and NASDAQ volume peaks and institutional algorithms deliver the most displacement.

Silver Bullet — Time-Based FVG Sniper

Setup Conditions

  1. 1
    Establish HTF Bias: Check 1H or 4H for market structure. Are we bullish (higher highs/lows) or bearish (lower highs/lows)? Mark premium/discount zones using Fibonacci.
  2. 2
    Identify Draw on Liquidity (DOL): Where is price likely heading? Previous session high/low, PDH/PDL, or an unfilled FVG on a higher timeframe.
  3. 3
    Wait for Silver Bullet Window: Only look for setups during the 3 time windows. Outside these hours, close your charts.
  4. 4
    Mark Session High/Low: At the start of the SB window, mark the current session's high and low. These are your liquidity targets.
Pro Tip

The 10-11 AM window is highest probability because it's 1 hour after NYSE opens. The initial 9:30-10:00 volatility creates the liquidity sweep, and the Silver Bullet window captures the real move that follows.

Entry Model

  1. 1
    Liquidity Sweep: Within the SB window, price must sweep a session high/low or PDH/PDL. This grabs stops and creates the fuel for the real move.
  2. 2
    Displacement: After the sweep, watch for a strong move in the opposite direction — big-bodied candles with momentum. This displacement creates the FVG.
  3. 3
    Identify the FVG: The first Fair Value Gap created by the displacement is your entry zone. It must form within the SB time window.
  4. 4
    Entry: Set a limit order at the FVG boundary closest to your trade direction. Bullish = top of FVG. Bearish = bottom of FVG.
  5. 5
    SL: Above/below the liquidity sweep wick. TP: Next internal liquidity level or the DOL identified in setup.

What is OTE?

Optimal Trade Entry is ICT's Fibonacci-based pullback entry. When price makes an impulsive move and retraces, the OTE zone (62%–79% retracement) is where institutions reload their positions. It's deep enough to offer a genuine discount but shallow enough that the trend isn't broken.

Think of it this way: if you're bullish and price just made a strong leg up, OTE tells you exactly where to buy the dip — not at random support, but at the mathematically optimal spot where smart money is accumulating.

The Key Fibonacci Levels

0.50 — Equilibrium 0.62 — OTE Start 0.705 — Sweet Spot 0.79 — OTE End
OTE Zone (62%–79%) — Bullish Pullback Entry

Setup Conditions

  1. 1
    Clear Impulse Leg: Price must have made a strong, clean move with displacement — not a choppy grind. The impulse creates the dealing range you'll draw Fibonacci on.
  2. 2
    Market Structure Confirmation: Bullish OTE needs a bullish MSS (break above a recent swing high). Bearish OTE needs a bearish MSS.
  3. 3
    Draw Fibonacci: Bullish = swing low to swing high. Bearish = swing high to swing low. Body-to-body (ignoring wicks) gives cleaner levels.
  4. 4
    Price Must Cross Below 50% (EQ): The retracement must go deeper than equilibrium. If price only pulls back to 40% and reverses, the OTE is not valid — the risk is too large relative to reward.
Pro Tip

The best OTE setups have an Order Block or FVG sitting right inside the 62-79% zone. When the OTE level aligns with a PD array, you get a double-confluence entry that institutions are likely using too.

Entry Model

  1. 1
    Wait for Retracement: Let price pull back into the OTE zone (62-79%). Don't jump in early at 50%.
  2. 2
    Find the PD Array: Inside the OTE zone, look for an Order Block or FVG. This is your precision entry point.
  3. 3
    Confirmation: Wait for a rejection candle (bullish engulfing, pin bar) or an LTF market structure shift inside the OTE zone.
  4. 4
    Entry: Enter at the OB/FVG retest. The 0.705 level (sweet spot) is the ideal entry.
  5. 5
    SL: Just below the swing low (bullish) or above the swing high (bearish) — the 100% Fibonacci level.
  6. 6
    TP1: -0.27 Fibonacci extension. TP2: -0.62 Fibonacci extension.
Min R:R — 1:2 | Sweet Spot Entry (0.705) often gives 1:3+
Common Mistake

Drawing Fibonacci on a weak, choppy leg instead of a clear impulse with displacement. OTE is a location tool, not a signal — context (market structure + liquidity) comes first, Fibonacci comes second.

What is Power of 3?

Power of 3 (PO3) is the macro framework that explains how every trading day unfolds. It breaks the day into three phases — Accumulation, Manipulation, Distribution — that map to specific sessions. While other strategies tell you where to enter, PO3 tells you why the market is moving and when each phase transitions.

Think of it as the Wyckoff cycle compressed into a single trading day. Asian session = accumulation. London open = manipulation. NY session = distribution. Once you see this rhythm, you stop getting trapped by fake moves.

Power of 3 — Bullish Day (Bearish Manipulation into Bullish Distribution)

The 3 Phases Explained

Phase 1: Accumulation

Happens during the Asian session (roughly 8 PM – 2 AM EST). Price moves sideways in a tight range. Smart money is quietly building positions near the daily open price. Retail traders see "nothing happening" and look away. That's the point — positions are being loaded without moving price.

What to look for: A clear horizontal range. Mark the high and low — these become the liquidity targets for the next phase.

Phase 2: Manipulation

Happens at the London open (2-5 AM EST). Price aggressively breaks one side of the accumulation range. On a bullish day, it sweeps below the Asian low. On a bearish day, it spikes above the Asian high. This is the Judas Swing — the fake move designed to trigger stops and trap breakout traders.

What to look for: A sweep of the Asian range high/low followed by a strong reversal with displacement. The manipulation leg creates FVGs and OBs for your entry.

Phase 3: Distribution

Happens during the NY session (8 AM – 2 PM EST). This is the REAL move. After trapping traders with the fake breakout, smart money drives price in the intended direction — toward the day's target (PDH/PDL, weekly level, or unfilled FVG). This phase typically forms 60-80% of the daily candle's body.

What to look for: Strong directional movement with follow-through. This is where you ride the trade to your TP.

Bullish vs Bearish Day

Bullish PO3

Asian range forms → London sweeps BELOW range (manipulation down) → NY drives price UP (distribution up). Daily candle opens near high, wicks down, closes near high.

Bearish PO3

Asian range forms → London sweeps ABOVE range (manipulation up) → NY drives price DOWN (distribution down). Daily candle opens near low, wicks up, closes near low.

Entry Model

You don't trade the accumulation or manipulation phases. You trade the transition from manipulation to distribution. Here's how:

  1. 1
    Establish Daily Bias: Use HTF (Daily/4H) to determine if the day is likely bullish or bearish. Look at the previous day's close, weekly structure, and draw on liquidity.
  2. 2
    Mark Asian Range: Identify the accumulation zone high/low during the Asian session (8 PM – 2 AM EST).
  3. 3
    Wait for the Sweep: During London open, watch for price to sweep one side of the Asian range. Bullish bias? Wait for a sweep BELOW the Asian low.
  4. 4
    Confirm MSS/Displacement: After the sweep, drop to 5m/15m and look for a Market Structure Shift in the direction of your bias. The displacement creates FVGs.
  5. 5
    Enter at FVG/OB: Enter on the retrace to the FVG or OB created by the displacement. SL below the manipulation low (bullish) or above the manipulation high (bearish).
  6. 6
    TP: Target the opposite side — PDH if bullish, PDL if bearish, or the next significant liquidity pool.
Target R:R — 1:2 to 1:4 | Distribution leg = the daily body
Critical Rule

If a lower-timeframe PO3 aligns with the higher-timeframe draw on liquidity, the distribution move tends to have clean follow-through. If it doesn't align, you're trading against the current — expect chop.

What is Fixed Range Volume Profile?

Most volume indicators show you how much was traded over TIME (those bars at the bottom of your chart). Volume Profile flips that — it shows how much was traded at each PRICE LEVEL, displayed as a horizontal histogram on the side of your chart.

The "Fixed Range" part means YOU choose the start and end points. You draw it over a specific price leg, consolidation, or session — and it reveals exactly where the heavy trading happened within that range.

Think of it as an X-ray of price action. Candles tell you what price did. Volume Profile tells you what traders actually COMMITTED money to.

Fixed Range Volume Profile — Key Components

The core principle: price gravitates toward areas of HIGH volume (where institutions transacted heavily) and moves quickly through areas of LOW volume (where there's no support/resistance).

The 5 Key Components

1. Point of Control (POC)

The price level with the highest traded volume in the entire range. Think of it as the "fairest price" where both buyers and sellers agreed the most. Price gets pulled back to the POC like a magnet.

Acts as strong S/RMagnet for retestsInstitutional acceptance

In an uptrend, POC acts as support on pullbacks. In a downtrend, it acts as resistance on rallies. A "Naked POC" (one that hasn't been retested yet) is especially powerful.

2. Value Area High (VAH)

The upper boundary of the Value Area — the price range containing 70% of total traded volume. Acts like a ceiling. If price is above VAH, bulls are firmly in control. If it rejects from VAH, sellers are stepping in.

3. Value Area Low (VAL)

The lower boundary of that same 70% zone. Acts like a floor. Buyers typically defend VAL. A clean break below it signals bearish control. Together, VAH and VAL create a "value fence" that contains the majority of market activity.

4. High Volume Nodes (HVN)

The thick, fat bulges in the histogram. These are prices where massive trading activity happened. HVNs act like speed bumps — price stalls, rotates, or bounces at these levels because many traders have positions there and will defend them.

Strong S/R zonesPrice consolidates hereUse as SL placement

5. Low Volume Nodes (LVN)

The thin, hollow gaps in the histogram. These are prices where almost nobody traded. LVNs are "air pockets" — price falls or rises through them fast because there's nothing to stop it. Use LVNs as targets (price will fly through them) not as entry points.

Key Insight

HVNs = speed bumps (price slows down). LVNs = air pockets (price accelerates). Your entries should be at HVNs. Your targets should be the LVNs or the next HVN on the other side.

Where to Draw Your Fixed Range

This is the most critical decision. FRVP should only be applied to completed price legs or ranges. Don't draw it on a move that's still developing — the levels will keep shifting and give unreliable references.

Best Anchoring Points

  1. 1
    Previous Day's Full Range: Open to close of yesterday's candle. Gives you yesterday's POC/VAH/VAL as today's key levels. This is the bread-and-butter for day traders.
  2. 2
    Completed Impulse Leg: Swing low to swing high (or vice versa). Reveals where institutions loaded positions during the move. Great for finding pullback entry levels.
  3. 3
    Consolidation Before Breakout: Draw it over the range/chop zone before a breakout. Shows the "acceptance zone" — the POC becomes the most important retest level if price returns.
  4. 4
    Asian Session Range: For intraday trading, anchor to the Asian session (8 PM – 2 AM EST). Reveals where overnight orders clustered. POC of Asia = key level for London/NY.
  5. 5
    Weekly Range: For swing trading, draw the FRVP on the full weekly candle. Gives macro institutional levels that hold for days.
3 Best FRVP Anchoring Methods
Pro Tip

After drawing your FRVP, look for horizontal price levels that align with the profile's VAH, VAL, POC, or HVNs. This "level stacking" approach ensures your S/R zones are validated by both price action AND volume — far more reliable than eyeballing.

The highest-probability FRVP setup. Price drifts away from the POC, then returns to retest it. In a trending market, POC acts as support (uptrend) or resistance (downtrend).

  1. 1

  2. 2
    Wait for price to pull back toward the POC.
  3. 3
    Look for rejection: Bullish engulfing, pin bar, or LTF Market Structure Shift at the POC.
  4. 4
    Enter in the direction of the prevailing trend.
  5. 5
    SL: 10-15 ticks beyond the POC or the next HVN behind you.
  6. 6

Strategy 2: Value Area Breakout

When price closes outside the Value Area, it signals the market is accepting new prices. This is a breakout-continuation setup.

  1. 1
    Draw FRVP on the consolidation range before the breakout.
  2. 2
    Wait for a candle to CLOSE above VAH (bullish) or below VAL (bearish) — not just wick past it.
  3. 3
    Confirm with volume surge on the breakout candle. Tiny candles that snap back inside = fakeout. Avoid.
  4. 4
    Enter on a retest of the broken VAH/VAL level (it flips from resistance to support or vice versa).
  5. 5
    SL: Back inside the Value Area. If it re-enters, the breakout failed.
  6. 6
    TP: Next major HVN, or a measured move equal to the Value Area width.

Strategy 3: LVN Breakout (Momentum Play)

LVNs are air pockets where almost nobody traded. When price enters an LVN, it accelerates because there's no volume to stop it. This is a momentum continuation setup.

  1. 1
    Identify an HVN (consolidation zone) sitting next to an LVN (thin gap in the profile).
  2. 2
    Wait for price to break out of the HVN and into the LVN.
  3. 3
    Enter in the direction of the breakout. Price will slice through the LVN quickly.
  4. 4
    SL: Back inside the HVN you just broke out of.
  5. 5
    TP: The next HVN on the other side of the LVN — price will stall there.
Best in: Momentum/trending | R:R: 1:2 to 1:4
Why It Works

HVNs are where traders have positions and will defend them. LVNs are empty zones — no positions to defend, no orders to absorb. When price leaves the crowded HVN and enters the empty LVN, it's like stepping off a busy road onto an open highway. Speed increases immediately.

What is SMT Divergence?

SMT stands for Smart Money Technique. SMT Divergence occurs when two correlated assets that normally move together suddenly move in opposite directions. One makes a new high while the other fails to confirm — or one makes a new low while the other doesn't. This "breakdown in sync" reveals that smart money is manipulating one of the assets to grab liquidity before the real move.

Think of it like two friends who always walk the same direction. If one suddenly turns around while the other keeps going, something is off. That "something" is institutional activity — and it signals a potential reversal.

Originally developed by ICT for NQ (Nasdaq) and ES (S&P 500), SMT Divergence has been proven across forex pairs, metals, and crypto.

Bullish SMT Divergence — Asset A Sweeps Low, Asset B Holds

Key insight: SMT Divergence is NOT an entry signal by itself — it's a bias tool. It tells you the DIRECTION. You then use other ICT tools (FVG, OB, MSS) for the actual entry.

Correlated Pairs to Watch

SMT only works between assets that have a high historical correlation. Using mismatched pairs will give meaningless signals. Here are the proven pairs organized by market:

Forex — Positive Correlation (Move Together)

EUR/USD ↔ GBP/USD AUD/USD ↔ NZD/USD

These pairs share the USD as the quote currency. When EUR/USD rises, GBP/USD usually rises too. If one makes a new high and the other doesn't — that's your divergence.

Forex — Negative Correlation (Move Opposite)

EUR/USD ↔ DXY (Dollar Index) GBP/USD ↔ DXY USD/JPY ↔ DXY (positive with DXY)

DXY moves inversely to EUR/USD and GBP/USD. If EUR/USD makes a lower low, DXY should make a higher high. If DXY fails to make a higher high — that's a bullish SMT divergence on EUR/USD.

Indices — Positive Correlation

NQ (Nasdaq 100) ↔ ES (S&P 500) ES ↔ YM (Dow Jones)

NQ and ES are the most popular pair for index SMT. Tech-heavy Nasdaq often leads or lags the broader S&P. If NQ makes a higher high but ES doesn't, NQ may be extended and due for a reversal.

Crypto — Positive Correlation

BTC (Bitcoin) ↔ ETH (Ethereum)

BTC and ETH typically move together. If BTC dumps to a new low but ETH holds a higher low, smart money may be accumulating ETH — signaling a bullish reversal for the entire crypto market.

Crypto — Negative Correlation

BTC ↔ DXY

When the dollar strengthens, crypto typically weakens. If BTC makes a lower low but DXY fails to make a higher high, the selling pressure in crypto may be exhausted.

Metals — Positive Correlation

XAU/USD (Gold) ↔ XAG/USD (Silver)

Gold and Silver move together. If Gold makes a higher high but Silver doesn't — Gold may be trapping breakout buyers before reversing.

Finding the Stronger/Weaker Asset

When you spot a divergence, trade the stronger asset. If EUR/USD makes a lower low but GBP/USD holds a higher low (bullish divergence), GBP/USD is the stronger pair — enter long on GBP/USD, not EUR/USD. You can also check EUR/GBP to confirm which pair has relative strength.

Bullish SMT Divergence

Occurs when both correlated assets are in a downtrend, and at a swing low:

  1. A
    Asset A makes a LOWER LOW — breaking below the previous swing low (liquidity grab below).
  2. B
    Asset B makes a HIGHER LOW — it fails to break its previous low. It holds strength.

This divergence suggests that the lower low on Asset A is a liquidity grab / stop hunt — not a genuine continuation. Smart money is accumulating, and a bullish reversal is likely.

Bullish SMT — EUR/USD sweeps low, GBP/USD holds = expect UP move

Bearish SMT Divergence

Occurs when both correlated assets are in an uptrend, and at a swing high:

  1. A
    Asset A makes a HIGHER HIGH — breaking above the previous swing high (liquidity grab above).
  2. B
    Asset B makes a LOWER HIGH — it fails to break its previous high. Momentum is weakening.

This divergence suggests the higher high on Asset A is a liquidity grab / stop hunt above resistance. Smart money is distributing, and a bearish reversal is likely.

Bearish SMT — NQ makes new high, ES fails = expect DOWN move

Negative Correlation SMT (DXY)

When using inversely correlated assets (like EUR/USD vs DXY), the logic flips:

  1. →
    Bullish EUR/USD SMT: EUR/USD makes a lower low, but DXY fails to make a higher high. The dollar weakness confirms the EUR sweep was fake.
  2. →
    Bearish EUR/USD SMT: EUR/USD makes a higher high, but DXY fails to make a lower low. The dollar strength underneath confirms the EUR push was a trap.
Pro Tip

DXY is the "truth detector." When your forex pair makes a new extreme but DXY doesn't confirm it with the opposite move, the market is lying. Trade accordingly.

How to Trade SMT Divergence

SMT Divergence is a directional bias tool, not a standalone entry signal. Here's the complete workflow:

Step-by-Step Process

  1. 1
    Open Two Charts Side by Side: Pull up your correlated pair (e.g., EUR/USD and GBP/USD) on the same timeframe. Sync them so you're comparing apples to apples.
  2. 2
    Identify Key Swing Points: Mark recent swing highs and swing lows on BOTH charts. Look for equal highs/lows or areas where liquidity is clustered (PDH/PDL, session highs/lows).
  3. 3
    Watch for the Divergence: When price approaches a key level, does one asset make a new extreme while the other fails? If yes — you have SMT Divergence.
  4. 4
    Determine Which Asset to Trade: Trade the STRONGER asset. Bullish SMT? Go long on the pair that held the higher low. Bearish SMT? Go short on the pair that made the lower high.
  5. 5
    Confirm with ICT Entry: Drop to LTF and look for a Market Structure Shift, FVG, or Order Block in the direction of your SMT bias. This is your actual entry trigger.
  6. 6
    Execute: Enter at the FVG/OB retest. SL beyond the swing point of the divergence. TP at the next liquidity pool or opposing swing.

What is Order Flow?

While ICT strategies analyze where smart money is likely positioning based on price action patterns, Order Flow lets you see the actual orders being placed in real time. It's like upgrading from reading footprints in the sand to watching someone walk live through a security camera.

Traditional charts show you WHAT happened (price went up/down). Order flow shows you WHO made it happen and HOW — were buyers aggressive? Were sellers absorbed? Is the volume genuine or fake?

Order flow focuses on aggressors — the traders using market orders to buy at the ask or sell at the bid. These are the participants who actually move price. Passive limit orders (makers) just sit and wait.

Traditional Chart vs Footprint — Same Candle, Different Intelligence

The 3 Pillars of Order Flow

1. Order Book / DOM (Depth of Market)

The DOM displays the live order book — all pending buy orders (bids) stacked on one side and sell orders (asks) on the other, like a two-sided ladder. The gap between the best bid and best ask is the spread.

What to watch: large order clusters (potential S/R walls), disappearing orders (spoofing — fake orders placed and quickly cancelled to deceive), and orders being consumed without price moving (absorption).

Large bid clusters = support Large ask clusters = resistance Narrow spread = high liquidity

Important limitation: The DOM only shows visible limit orders. Institutional players use iceberg orders — large orders broken into small visible chunks to avoid detection. What you see on the DOM is often just the tip of the iceberg.

2. Footprint Charts

The footprint chart is the X-ray of every candle. Instead of showing just open/high/low/close, it shows the volume traded at the bid and ask at each individual price level inside the candle.

Three types:

  1. B/A
    Bid/Ask Footprint: Shows both buy and sell volume at each price. The most detailed view. Reveals exactly where buyers lifted offers and sellers hit bids.
  2. Δ
    Delta Footprint: Shows the NET difference (ask minus bid) at each price. Positive = buyers dominated. Negative = sellers dominated. Fastest to read.
  3. VP
    Volume Profile Footprint: Shows total volume per price level regardless of direction. Identifies the POC (highest volume level) inside each candle.

3. Delta & Cumulative Volume Delta (CVD)

Delta = Ask volume minus Bid volume for a single candle. Positive delta means buyers were more aggressive. Negative delta means sellers dominated.

CVD tracks delta over time across the entire session. It's like a running scoreboard of buyer vs seller aggression.

The most powerful signal is delta divergence:

Bearish Delta Divergence

Price makes higher highs but delta makes lower highs → buyers are weakening → expect bearish reversal.

Bullish Delta Divergence

Price makes lower lows but delta makes higher lows → sellers are exhausting → expect bullish reversal.

Key Insight

A green candle with negative delta means aggressive sellers are actually dominating despite the bullish appearance. A red candle with positive delta means hidden buying is happening despite the bearish look. These contradictions are where the real institutional signals hide.

The 5 Core Order Flow Patterns

1. Absorption

The most powerful pattern. Large limit orders soak up aggressive market orders without price moving. Example: price keeps hitting resistance with heavy buy volume but won't break — hidden sellers are absorbing every buy. The price stalls, then reverses.

Signal: heavy volume + no price movementResult: reversal

2. Stacked Imbalances

When 3-5 consecutive price levels all show lopsided volume in the same direction (e.g., ask volume vastly exceeding bid at every level), that's stacked imbalances. This signals strong institutional conviction — trade in that direction for continuation.

Signal: 3+ levels of one-sided volumeResult: continuation

3. Exhaustion

Price reaches a new extreme (high or low) but volume is declining. Fewer participants are stepping in to push further. The fuel is running out. The move is about to stall or reverse.

Signal: new extreme + declining volumeResult: reversal

4. Delta Divergence

Price moves in one direction but delta moves in the opposite. It's the order flow equivalent of SMT Divergence — the "hidden truth" beneath the candle. CVD rising while price is flat = accumulation (smart money buying quietly). CVD falling while price rises = distribution (smart money selling into strength).

Signal: price vs delta conflictResult: early reversal warning

5. Trapped Traders

A breakout occurs with volume, then price immediately reverses. Those who chased the breakout are now trapped. Their stop losses become fuel for the reversal — price accelerates in the opposite direction as stops cascade.

Signal: breakout + instant reversalResult: fast reversal (stop cascade)
